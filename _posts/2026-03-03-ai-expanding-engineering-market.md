Everyone seems convinced that AI is going to crater demand for software engineers. The logic is straightforward: AI writes code now, so companies will need fewer engineers, so the market shrinks. Supply up, demand down, salaries follow.

I'm not so sure. I think it might do the opposite — though not in the way most people are imagining.

## The companies nobody talks about

When people argue about AI and engineering jobs, the mental model is almost always big tech. Google, Meta, Amazon. And maybe that's right — maybe those companies hire leaner as tooling improves. I don't know.

But those companies are not most companies.

Most companies in the world are not tech companies. They're not even trying to be. They're manufacturers, service providers, distributors, healthcare practices, regional retailers. Many of them have a website from 2014. Some of them don't have a website at all. A lot of them have internal processes held together by spreadsheets and tribal knowledge and the one person who's been there for fifteen years and just *knows*.

These companies aren't on the engineering job market because the math never worked. Hiring a good software engineer is expensive — not just the salary, but the organizational overhead, the dependency you're creating, the ROI you need to justify it. For a company doing a few million dollars a year in revenue, that calculation rarely pencils out.

I think that's what's about to change.

## The denominator shifts

When a software engineer using modern AI tools can do the work that used to require a small team, the ROI of hiring that engineer changes. The cost is roughly the same. The output isn't.

A solo engineer at a company that's never had engineering talent before can now build and maintain things that would have taken multiple people not that long ago. The company gets more value. The engineer can command good compensation. And the hire finally makes sense in a way it probably never did.

I think there are a *lot* of companies in this position. Companies that have wanted to move up the technology stack for years — from no web presence to one, from a basic website to actual e-commerce, from e-commerce to real data infrastructure — but couldn't justify the talent to get there. AI might be the thing that finally makes that possible.

## What this might mean for engineers

I find myself grappling with the doom framing a lot, especially in the day-to-day. It's hard not to when the tools are getting as good as they are. The anxiety is real and I don't want to paper over it.

But when I zoom out, I keep coming back to something that feels hopeful, even if I can't be certain about it: engineering talent is heavily concentrated right now. The best engineers cluster at the biggest tech companies because that's where the leverage and the compensation are. Smaller companies can't compete.

If AI genuinely changes the leverage equation across the board, that changes where it makes sense to be an engineer. Not 500 engineers at one company, but maybe 50 engineers each at 10 companies that never had a single one before. The same talent, distributed differently. The same demand, spread across more of the economy.

Whether that plays out the way I'm imagining, I genuinely don't know. But it seems worth hoping for.

## The part that's less comfortable

I don't think all engineers are equally positioned for this. The engineers who will probably be fine are the ones who bring judgment, domain knowledge, and the ability to translate messy business problems into software solutions. The ones who are harder to replace than the tools.

The engineers whose entire value proposition is "I can write code" — that's the part that's actually at risk. Because yes, AI is commoditizing that, and I don't think it's wrong to say so.

The honest version of the hopeful story I'm telling isn't "everyone will be fine." It's more like: the market might be bigger than people think, and the engineers who thrive in it will look a little different than the ones who thrived before.

That's worth thinking about now, while there's still time to think about it.
