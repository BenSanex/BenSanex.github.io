With Venezuela in the news, I wanted to write up my thoughts in a longer form post. I realized that I didn't continue my series of writing up the trip, so I'm collecting some highlights here in one post before getting into commentary.

#### Catamaran

Highlight of the trip. Took a catamaran out to a sandbar from Tucacas with 25 friends/family. Ate a ton of supposedly fresh sea snails and oysters. Guys take a boat out to the beaches or sandbars and sell them to the tourists. Drank a lot of rum. The boat docked there for a few hours and we partied until we were tired, then floated back to shore. Grilled at the apartment after.

#### Cata

Famous beach over the mountains from Maracay. 2-3 hour drive through the mountains and jungle. Road was tight and curvy, fun but scary, stay out of the way of buses! Beach was incredible, not busy, a little windy and big waves. There are restaurants right on the beach with tables and umbrellas set up. If you buy food and drinks from them you get a spot. We spent ~$100 with them and felt like VIPs. They helped us park, carry gear, washed the sand off our feet at the car, and then reserved us a spot for the following day.

Spent the night in a posada, basically a motel/B&B, incredible breakfast. Pool in the courtyard area, Scarlett slept outside. There was another family having a birthday party and they shared cake with us. It was being renovated and looks like now they have all 3 floors of rooms open. When we were there it was just the bottom floor, like 4 rooms.

#### Los Roques

Ok, real highlight of the trip. Once in a lifetime experience. Took a tiny plane to a small island archipelago off the coast north of Caracas. 35 minute flight. There are no cars, no paved roads. Someone from the posada where you are staying picks you up from the airport with a handcart and walks you to where you're staying.

All the meals are included, so during the day you tell them where you want to go and then hop on a lancha (small boat) and they take you to a beach. It's a 45 minute ride through basically open ocean on this tiny fiberglass boat with 10 people on it. Genesis was terrified and found a life jacket. We saw flying fish on the way, it was surreal.

The premier spot is called Cayo de Agua. It's a small island with the softest sand, bluest water, and only a couple people. Seriously, the sand is like bread flour. We spent a couple hours there. You can walk through a sandbar to a separate island with a lighthouse.

After spending time there they took us to a sea turtle sanctuary. I needed to use the bathroom after drinking lots of rum on the beach, but they said they couldn't stop otherwise the boat would capsize, so I had to hold it for the 30 minutes it took to get there. We held a couple of sea turtles and then they took us to find starfish in the shallows. Found some in the water, took underwater pictures with Genesis' phone, which then started complaining about water in the charging port and I was terrified I ruined the phone.

After getting back they made us dinner at the posada. The cook was a young girl just out of culinary school and her cooking was phenomenal. First night was pasta with fish, second night was steak with some kind of green sauce.

There is a lighthouse on the top of the main island, Gran Roque, that you can walk to for sunset. We were not prepared for a hike but it was just "like right there" so we walked up in flip flops. 45 minutes later we barely caught sunset. Genesis was mad because it was a drunken silly decision and a way harder hike than we planned for, but it was very pretty.

After dinner our group from the day wanted to keep things going so we went to the bar on the island, where they were doing karaoke kinda. It was a show, some of the workers were singing and quite good, then they brought up a guy staying there who used to be in a popular Venezuelan boy band. It was a lot of fun, but we got tired and went back to bed around midnight. Unfortunately the bar and music was outside and right behind our hotel. They kept going until 2am but we were so tired we did sleep. Last day they took us to a nearby beach where we said goodbye to our new friends before going back to the airport and heading back to Caracas.

> **Airport note:** Venezuela requires you to get to the airport 3 hours before your flight. They check everyone in at once and then close check-ins. If you're late, sorry, doesn't matter if the flight doesn't board for 2 hours, you're out of luck. They don't have computers at check-in, they print off a manifest and then you line up and talk to 4 different people who approve you to get on the plane, at least for international flights.

We missed our flight home and because they weren't flying that often had to stay another week. I was a little tired of the food at this point. It's weird how bad I was craving a ham and cheese sandwich, but Craftjack had just been shut down so I didn't really need to go back to work. Hard to complain, especially in retrospect.

#### Colonia Tovar

Small German town in the mountains roughly halfway between Caracas and Maracay. You drive about an hour and a half up into the mountains, not a jungle like on the way to Cata. Was pretty empty when we were there, they brew their own beer and are famous for their strawberry farms. Ate a lot of strawberry stuff. It was founded in 1843 so the Germanness is very diluted but still has the style and tries to have German food. Was funny having sauerkraut there. I guess fermented foods don't go along with the Venezuelan appetite so it was pretty weak.

I did attempt to work a little bit but Scarlett's internet was absolutely terrible, so a neighbor had an unused condo in the neighborhood hooked up with internet. I spent a couple days in there since technically I was supposed to be back.

#### Food

Street hotdogs are tasty and cheap but unreliable, both of us got sick on separate occasions. They put ham on a lot of things where we would be more like salami or pepperoni. Empanadas are greasy, cheap and more of a breakfast food. We found a cart selling excellent hamburgers so went a couple nights there. We found a shawarma restaurant which was very tasty but I got sick afterwards so we didn't go back. Parrilla on an early night was incredible, just a giant mountain of meats and it was like $50. I forget if I talked about beers. The big one is Polar, but it's essentially water, I preferred Zulia. There's one more that I don't remember that was not too bad.
